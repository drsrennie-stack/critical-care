02

Audience and positioning

Who this is for

Students currently enrolled in a college-level Anatomy & Physiology course who are currently failing or close to failing. Most commonly pre-nursing and pre-health students whose academic progression depends on passing A&P. Open to students at any institution, any A&P course variant, any instructor.

Who this is not for

Students who are passing and want to do better (this is not an enrichment program). Students who want content tutoring on specific A&P topics (this is not a tutoring service). Students who have already failed the course and are not currently enrolled (a different program would fit better).

The core problem the program solves

Failing students rarely lack information. They lack structures: reliable accountability, working study methods, foundation knowledge from earlier units, strategic clarity about what to do with limited time, and often the emotional regulation to do any of the above consistently. Most study support is preventative and assumes a functional student. Critical Care is designed for students whose structures have collapsed and who need them rebuilt quickly.

Key positioning statements

What we are

A structured recovery methodology borrowed from clinical critical care, applied to academic recovery.

What we are not

A tutoring service, a replacement for the student's A&P course, or a guarantee of a passing grade.

Core mechanism

Accountability structure through the weekly STAT cycle and team-based case work, not additional content instruction.

Differentiation

Clinical framing, physician-designed, team-based, and built specifically for students in crisis rather than students in maintenance.

03

The STAT cycle

The operational rhythm of every week. Students learn to think in STAT from week 1. The cycle is the program's core intellectual property and the mnemonic students use to orient themselves week to week.

The weekly operational cycle

STAT

Status. Triage. Action. Track. The mantra students carry through the program and into every course after.

S
Status
Individual audit completed before the live session. SOAP-structured honest self-assessment.
T
Triage
Team-based case discussion during the live session. Diagnose what matters most.
A
Action
Personal orders for the week. 7-day plan, 24-hour action, smallest next step.
T
Track
Progress note in next week's Status. Did you do what you said you would do.

Phase details

S — Status (pre-session, individual)

Completed before each live session, on the student's own time. Structured as a SOAP note on themselves (see section 04). Students submit the Status audit to their facilitator. Scored for completion and honesty, not performance.

Time required: 15 to 20 minutes per week.

T — Triage (live session, team)

During the weekly live session, teams receive a common case of a composite failing student. Teams diagnose the case student's situation, identify what matters most, and build a recovery plan for that student. Facilitator guides the discussion and surfaces the reasoning frameworks that will transfer to each student's own situation.

Time required: 40 to 50 minutes of the live session.

A — Action (post-session, individual)

Immediately after the Triage discussion, students work individually on the Orders phase. They apply the team's reasoning to their own situation, write a 7-day plan, commit to a 24-hour action, and identify the study method or assumption most likely to be wrong.

Time required: 20 to 25 minutes at the end of the live session, plus ongoing execution through the week.

T — Track (next week's Status, individual)

The first section of next week's Status audit is the Track phase. Students report whether they executed this week's Action commitments. Track is what closes the loop and creates the behavioral ratchet. The facilitator and team see this data, which is what makes accountability structural rather than aspirational.

04

SOAP inside Status

The Status phase is internally structured as a SOAP note. SOAP is the standard clinical documentation framework used by every medical and nursing professional. Teaching students to write a weekly SOAP note on themselves is both pedagogically rigorous and clinically transferable.

The SOAP structure within Status

Section Purpose Example questions
S — Subjective How did this week feel. What got in the way. Honest one-sentence reflection. What is the truest thing you can say about this past week?
O — Objective The numbers. Current grade, hours studied, assessments completed, instructor contact. What is your current grade? How many hours did you actually study?
A — Assessment What does the data mean. What is the single biggest thing hurting your grade. If nothing changes, what is the most likely reason you'll fail the next assessment?
P — Plan Preview of where your Action phase will focus this week. What you expect to commit to. Coming into today's session, what do you think you most need to work on?
When to introduce SOAP explicitly

The audit templates use SOAP structure from week 1, but the SOAP framework is not named explicitly until week 3 or 4, when students have experienced enough Status cycles to appreciate the frame. Introducing SOAP too early adds cognitive load. Introducing it too late misses the pedagogical opportunity.

Weekly structure

Week Theme Primary work
Week 1 Triage and team formation Intake, team charter, grade audit, failure mode diagnostic. No case this week.
Week 2 Stop the bleeding Morgan case. Study method diagnostics. First full STAT cycle.
Week 3 Foundation repair, selective Jordan case. Load-bearing prerequisites. Introduce SOAP framework explicitly.
Week 4 The midterm hinge Exam strategy and exam wrapper. Recovery from a failed exam.
Week 5 Point recovery Institutional navigation. Extra credit audit. Self-advocacy with instructors.
Week 6 Consolidation and integration Cross-system reasoning. Teach-backs within teams.
Week 7 Final exam runway Structured final push. Mock final in team. Finals week logistics.
Week 8 Definitive care Final exam execution. Cumulative program assessment. Portfolio and debrief.

Session structure (every week)

Each 90-minute live session follows a consistent structure so students always know what to expect.

Time Segment What happens
0-5 min Opening ritual One-word check-in per team member. Facilitator opens the week.
5-50 min Triage: team case discussion Four-part case work on this week's composite student case.
50-65 min Whole-cohort debrief Teams share findings. Facilitator connects patterns across cases.
65-85 min Action: personal application Individual Orders work informed by the case discussion.
85-90 min Closing ritual One-sentence takeaway per team member. Preview of next week.
06

Team architecture

Formation principles

  • Instructor-assigned, not self-selected. Friend groups fail together. Intentional heterogeneity outperforms affinity.
  • Heterogeneous by failure mode. Mix students with different primary failure patterns (content gap, study method, time crunch, affective crisis) so teams can see multiple recovery pathways.
  • Heterogeneous by current standing. A mix of students closer to passing and students further from passing. Students closer to the line provide modeling; students further provide urgency.
  • Homogeneous by schedule. Team meetings outside the main session depend on schedule compatibility. Teams that cannot meet cannot study together.
  • Attention to safety. Students flagged in intake as needing specific accommodations (for example, recovery from harassment, accessibility needs, language considerations) are placed into teams thoughtfully.

Team charter

Each team produces a charter in week 1. The charter is a one-page document that states what the team commits to, what it will not tolerate, how it will communicate outside sessions, and what the team will do if a member disappears. The charter is revisited at weeks 4 and 8, and becomes the artifact teams use if they convert into a study group after the program ends.

Trust scaffold

Teams build trust over the 8 weeks, not on day one. The Status audit includes a weekly trust check where students indicate what they are comfortable sharing with their team. Facilitators use this data to calibrate how much personal material enters team discussions. By week 5 or 6, most teams are sharing meaningful personal material voluntarily.

Peer accountability

Peer accountability is introduced progressively. Weeks 1 to 3: audits are private, accountability is between student and facilitator. Weeks 4 to 5: formative peer evaluation introduced, focused on team process. Weeks 6 to 8: teams share Status summaries at the team's discretion.

07

Case-based pedagogy

The central pedagogical move

The program's Triage phase uses composite cases of struggling students. Teams work the case of a student other than themselves. This is the program's central pedagogical mechanism. Students can reason clearly about a hypothetical failing student in ways they cannot yet reason about themselves. The case becomes a mirror they are willing to look into. By the time they reach the Action phase, they have already developed the analytic frame; applying it to themselves is a smaller cognitive and emotional lift.

Case library design

  • One case per week for weeks 2 through 7. Weeks 1 and 8 do not have cases.
  • Each case introduces a specific diagnostic or strategic challenge that parallels the cohort's current situation.
  • Cases increase in sophistication: early cases surface obvious problems (wrong study methods), later cases surface subtle problems (missing foundations, exam strategy errors, integration failures).
  • Characters and details rotate across cohorts to prevent students from knowing cases in advance through alumni contact.
  • Target library: 16 to 20 cases for the full product, allowing rotation across cohorts.

Established cases

  • Week 2 — Morgan. Failing student doing the wrong things with high effort. Lesson: method repair.
  • Week 3 — Jordan. Failing student doing the right things but with missing foundations. Lesson: selective foundation repair.

Weeks 4 through 7 cases to be drafted during pilot development.

08

Product ladder

Four tiers serving different student situations and budgets. Every program tier has real human accountability built in. Only the Toolkit tier is fully self-paced, and it does not claim to be a program.

Tier 1: Cohort (flagship)

Full 8-week cohort-based program. Team structure, live facilitated sessions, weekly audits, cumulative assessment, certificate. Positioned as the recommended option for most students. The most effective format and the leverage product economically.

Tier 2: 1:1 Coaching (premium)

Same curriculum delivered as individualized coaching. Weekly 45 to 60 minute video sessions with a coach. Positioned for students whose schedules or circumstances require individual work. Explicitly framed as not necessarily superior to cohort for most students; cohort benefits from peer support that 1:1 cannot provide.

Tier 3: Guided (accessible)

Asynchronous delivery. Students submit weekly Status audits and Action plans and receive written feedback within 48 hours. No live sessions, no team. Accountability through submission deadlines and human review. Positioned for students who cannot attend synchronous sessions or who cannot afford the cohort.

Tier 4: Toolkit (materials only)

One-time purchase of curriculum materials as a self-study resource. Explicitly positioned as materials, not as a program. No certificate, no facilitator support. Honest pricing for students who want the tools but are not ready for structured program participation.

Pricing rationale

  • Cohort is leverage product. Priced to be accessible to most students while valuing expert delivery appropriately.
  • 1:1 intentionally priced significantly higher than cohort. 1:1 uses scarcest resource (human time) at 1:1 ratio and must be priced to reflect that. Premium pricing also signals the tier is for specific circumstances, not the default.
  • Guided priced accessibly, with thin margins at the lower end. Viable at volume because it has less scheduling constraint.
  • Toolkit priced as materials, not as a program. No promises about outcomes.

Scholarship and access

Each cohort includes 2 scholarship slots at 50% off for students with documented financial hardship. Additional access pathways: institutional grant funding (Track 1), employer tuition support, and payment plans for the Cohort tier (2 or 3 installments).

Delivery tracks

Track 1
Institutional

Program delivered at Dr. Rennie's institution as additional-FTE teaching arrangement. Students pay tuition, potentially covered by institutional grants. MedMasters retains curriculum IP. Instructor (Dr. Rennie) paid by institution. Serves students enrolled at that specific institution.

Track 2
MedMasters Direct

Program delivered as MedMasters Critical Care product. Students pay MedMasters directly. Serves students at any institution. Does not solicit Dr. Rennie's own enrolled students. Revenue accrues to MedMasters LLC.

Ethical boundaries between tracks

  • Dr. Rennie does not refer her own enrolled students to Track 2. Referrals go to Track 1 or to institutional resources.
  • Track 2 marketing does not target Dr. Rennie's home institution.
  • Both tracks disclosed to institution through standard outside employment channels.
  • Track 2 exists independently. If institution declines Track 1 arrangement, Track 2 continues.

Future tracks

Track 3: Licensed. Institutions license the curriculum and deliver it with their own certified facilitators. Targeted for year 3 launch.

Track 4: Certified Facilitator Network. Individual facilitators certified to deliver the program at their own institutions or privately. Targeted for year 2 to 3 launch.

11

Facilitator model

Core insight

The program's curriculum is academic recovery methodology, not A&P content. Facilitators do not need to be A&P experts. They need to be skilled facilitators of the STAT cycle and the case-based methodology. This is what makes the program scalable beyond Dr. Rennie's personal delivery capacity.

Facilitator profile

Ideally someone who understands both preventative and rescue techniques for academic performance. Candidates include:

  • Academic coaches and learning center staff
  • Graduate students in education, counseling psychology, or higher education
  • Former teachers who have left K-12 or higher ed
  • Certified instructional coaches
  • Eventually: MedMasters Critical Care alumni who succeeded in the program

Facilitator boundaries

  • Facilitators do not answer A&P content questions. They redirect students to their course resources.
  • Facilitators do not critique the student's A&P instructor.
  • Facilitators facilitate the STAT cycle, discuss cases, provide written feedback on Actions, hold accountability.
  • Facilitators recognize and escalate concerns beyond their scope (mental health, accommodations, academic integrity).

Quality control

  • Certification training required before running any cohort (targeted: 20 to 30 hours).
  • Observation of first 2 to 3 sessions by Dr. Rennie or senior facilitator.
  • Per-facilitator outcome tracking with benchmarks.
  • Annual re-certification with program updates and continuing education.
  • Anonymous student feedback channels separate from the facilitator relationship.
12

Assessment and credential

Program completion requirements

  • Intake survey submitted before week 1.
  • Weekly Status audit submitted for all 8 weeks (one excused absence permitted).
  • Attendance at live sessions (one excused absence permitted, re-entry protocol for second absence).
  • Action commitments documented each week.
  • Cumulative program assessment completed in week 8.
  • Program portfolio completed in week 8.

Cumulative assessment

A comprehensive A&P assessment administered in week 8. Approximately 60 to 100 questions covering the major domains of a standard A&P course. Designed to have real rigor so that the credential signals genuine mastery. Multiple parallel forms to be developed for licensing tier.

The certificate

Students who complete the program requirements receive a MedMasters Critical Care Certificate of Completion. The certificate documents what the student accomplished and provides evidence of mastery that can be used in advocacy conversations with their institution. The certificate does not claim to recover the student's A&P grade. That remains in the hands of the student's instructor and institution.

13

Commitment contract

Every enrolled student signs a commitment contract during Section 1 of the course, before the intake survey. The contract is a program commitment contract, not a grade recovery contract.

What the student commits to

  • Weekly Status audit completed honestly and on time for all 8 weeks.
  • Attendance at all 8 live sessions (one excused absence built in).
  • Completion of all four STAT phases each week.
  • Execution of stated weekly Action commitments.
  • Cumulative assessment at end of program.
  • End-of-program portfolio.
  • Honest engagement, not performance.

What the program commits to

  • Facilitated weekly sessions for 8 weeks.
  • Team structure and case-based learning.
  • Instructor feedback on weekly Actions.
  • Cumulative assessment and certificate of completion.
  • Documentation student can use to advocate for grade recovery mechanisms at their institution.
  • Re-entry clause: one documented re-entry after a missed week.
  • Refund policy: documented and honored.

What the program does not promise

  • A passing grade in the student's A&P course.
  • Completion of the student's A&P course by any specific date.
  • Admission to any specific subsequent program (nursing, medical, etc.).
  • Any outcome beyond what the student's own execution produces.

Brand and design

Brand positioning

MedMasters Critical Care is a sub-brand of MedMasters Collaborative with its own distinct visual identity. The broader MedMasters brand is calm, aspirational, and preventative in tone (sage and navy). Critical Care is high-stakes, serious, and urgent (navy and gold). The palette reflects the emotional positioning.

Visual system

  • Primary colorDeep navy (#0A1F3D)
  • Accent colorBrushed gold (#B8904D) / Deep gold (#8F6E38) / Bright gold (#D4A962)
  • BackgroundWarm white (#FAFAF7)
  • TypographyPlus Jakarta Sans (all weights)
  • Accessibility targetWCAG AAA where feasible, AA minimum
  • Elevation systemFour levels: raised / accent-ruled / tinted / feature
  • Design principleRestrained sophistication. Color as emphasis, not fill.
